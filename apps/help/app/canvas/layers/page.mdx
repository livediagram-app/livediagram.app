Every tab starts with a single layer, and for many diagrams that's all you need. When a tab grows busy (a background grid, the main content, a pass of review notes), layers let you split it into named bands you can hide, lock, and restack independently, just like an image editor.

## The Layers panel

Open the panel with the **Layers button next to the zoom controls** (bottom-right). On a phone, or in the minimal panel layout, Layers lives in the compact dock row at the top-right instead, and opens as a popover.

Each row is one layer, top of the stack first, and shows:

- an **eye** to hide or show the layer,
- a **mini preview** of just that layer's elements, framed against the whole tab so you can see where its content sits,
- the layer's **name** (double-click to rename it inline), with a padlock mark when the layer is locked,
- an **ellipsis** opening the layer's menu.

Click a row to make that layer **active** and drag rows to restack the bands. **Hovering a row temporarily shows only that layer** on the canvas, so you can check what lives where without touching visibility (switch this off in the panel's gear menu, which also resets the panel's position). The footer can **add a layer** (it lands directly above the active one), **delete the active layer**, or **merge the active layer** with the one above or below it (the neighbour keeps its name and settings).

**Right-click a row (or click its ellipsis)** for the full menu, which opens beside the panel: rename and delete up top, then Layer (an **opacity slider** for the whole layer, Bring to Top, Send to Back, and **Hide Others**), Content (**lock** and **clear**), and Merge. Clearing empties the layer but keeps it around.

One nicety: a layer still called "Layer 2" adopts the first name you finish typing onto one of its elements, so stacks name themselves as you work. Deleting a layer that still has elements asks you to confirm first, because the elements go with it; undo brings both back. The last remaining layer can't be deleted.

The panel floats like the others: drag it to any corner ([panel docking](/help/user-interface/panel-layout/)) and collapse it back into its button when you're done.

## The active layer

Exactly one layer is always active, and **everything you add lands on it**: palette shapes, text, arrows, pastes, imports, AI-built elements. While the active layer is hidden or locked, adding is paused so you never create something you can't immediately see or touch.

## Stacking, front, and back

Layers are stacking bands: everything on a higher layer paints above everything on a lower one. **Bring to Front** and **Send to Back** work on layers too: Bring to Front moves the selection onto the top layer, creating a fresh one if the top layer holds anything else, and Send to Back mirrors that at the bottom. A layer those buttons empty out is cleaned up automatically, so casual reordering never litters the panel.

To place elements deliberately, select them, right-click, and pick a layer from the **Move to layer** tiles in the Layer category; the whole selection moves there. (They appear once the tab has more than one layer.)

## Hiding and locking

- A **hidden** layer's elements disappear from the canvas for everyone (collaborators, viewers, and embeds included) and can't be selected or caught by a marquee.
- A **locked** layer's elements stay visible but can't be selected, moved, edited, or deleted, like per-element [locking](/help/canvas/locking/) applied to the whole band.

## Layers and exports

Exports match what you see: hidden layers are left out of PNG, SVG, PDF, and Mermaid exports, and out of shared thumbnails. If you want a hidden layer included in an image export anyway, the export options screen offers a **Hidden layers** toggle whenever at least one layer is hidden.

<Note>
  Layers are saved with the tab and sync live, so everyone in the diagram sees the same stack. Which
  layer is <em>active</em> is yours alone; a collaborator can work on a different layer at the same
  time.
</Note>

<Tip>
  Keep review feedback on its own layer. Reviewers can sketch and annotate freely, you can hide the
  layer for a clean export, and one delete clears the whole pass when it's dealt with.
</Tip>
